Case 1: Serbian Restaurant Venture

Business Context

Danijela is planning to open a restaurant in Belgrade, Serbia. She has 8 years of experience working in various restaurants, including 3 years as a sous chef and 2 years in management. She has saved €50,000 to invest and can potentially secure an additional €30,000 loan from a local bank.

Case 1: Serbian Restaurant Venture

Market Context

  • Restaurant market in Belgrade consists of approximately 35% traditional Serbian restaurants, 25% international chains, 20% cafés with food service, and 20% specialty cuisines
  • Customer segments include locals (65%), business travelers (15%), and tourists (20%)
  • Average restaurant profit margins are 8-12% with high staff turnover (25% annually)
  • Food costs have increased 15% in the past year, while average menu prices have only increased 7%
  • Prime locations in Belgrade have rental costs of €25-35 per square meter per month
  • Belgrade tourism has grown 18% in the past two years
  • Traditional restaurants have loyal, older customer base (40+ years)
  • Growing interest in fusion and international cuisine among younger demographics (25-40 years)
  • Several international chains have entered the market in the past 3 years

Case 2: Egyptian Agricultural Operation

Business Context

Ahmed is looking to establish a farming operation in Egypt. He has access to 15 acres of land in the Nile Delta region and $75,000 in capital for investment. He has a background in agricultural engineering and previously worked for a government agricultural research center for 5 years.

Market Context

  • Egyptian agriculture contributes approximately 12% to GDP and employs 25% of the workforce
  • Government provides subsidies for certain traditional crops (wheat, rice, cotton)
  • Water scarcity is increasing due to climate change and upstream dam projects on the Nile River
  • Large industrial farms (100+ acres) dominate the production of traditional crops
  • Export market for organic and specialty crops to Europe is growing at 20% annually
  • Modern irrigation technology can reduce water usage by 30-60% but requires significant investment
  • Growing urban middle class is increasing demand for premium and organic produce domestically
  • Supply chain is fragmented with multiple intermediaries between farmers and end consumers
  • Regional instability affects export market access and creates price volatility

Strategic Questions to Address

  1. What type of market structure exists for different agricultural products, and which segment offers the best opportunity?
  2. Using PESTLE analysis, what are the critical external factors affecting this venture?
  3. How might Ahmed create competitive advantage through vertical integration or specialization?
  4. Apply the GE/McKinsey matrix to evaluate different crop/product opportunities.
  5. How would a focus/segmentation strategy differ from a cost leadership approach in this context?

Case 3: Canadian Digital Services Firm

Business Context

Jordan runs a digital marketing agency in Canada serving small to medium-sized businesses. The company has been operating for 4 years, has 8 full-time employees, and generates approximately $650,000 in annual revenue. Current services include social media management, content creation, and basic SEO services.

Market Context

  • Digital marketing industry is highly fragmented with thousands of small agencies and freelancers
  • Large marketing conglomerates are acquiring successful boutique agencies
  • AI tools are automating many basic digital marketing tasks that were previously billable services
  • Average client retention is 14 months before companies bring services in-house or switch providers
  • Economic uncertainty has led to 20% reduction in marketing budgets for many small businesses
  • Industry specialization yields 30% higher retention rates and 25% higher profit margins
  • Clients increasingly demand measurable ROI and performance-based pricing models
  • Remote work has expanded the talent pool but also increased competitive pressure from low-cost regions
  • Specialized services (e.g., influencer marketing, SaaS marketing) command premium prices

Strategic Questions to Address

  1. How would you characterize the competitive advantage of Jordan's agency currently?
  2. Using Porter's Five Forces, analyze the digital marketing industry.
  3. What growth strategies (diversification, horizontal/vertical integration) would be most appropriate?
  4. Apply the suitability, feasibility, and acceptability framework to evaluate a potential specialized niche strategy.
  5. How can Jordan use the strategic hierarchy concept to align various aspects of the business?

Case 4: Sustainable Products Manufacturer

Business Context

Lin has founded a small manufacturing company that produces environmentally-friendly household products. The company has been operating for three years with 12 employees and annual revenue of $1.2 million. Current product lines include biodegradable cleaning supplies, reusable alternatives to disposable items, and energy-efficient small appliances.

Market Context

  • Consumer awareness of environmental issues has increased 35% in the past 5 years
  • Large corporations are rapidly introducing "green" product lines with substantial marketing budgets
  • Sustainable products typically command a 20-40% price premium over conventional alternatives
  • Raw material costs for eco-friendly components are 30-50% higher than conventional materials
  • Retail partners demand 40-50% margins and promotional support to stock products
  • Direct-to-consumer sales through e-commerce have grown 60% in two years
  • Regulations around environmental claims are becoming stricter with penalties for "greenwashing"
  • Loyal customer base (approximately 30,000) with 65% repeat purchase rate
  • Supply chain disruptions have increased manufacturing lead times by 35%

Strategic Questions to Address

  1. What is the market structure Lin is operating in, and how is it evolving?
  2. Analyze the internal and external environment using SWOT and PESTLE frameworks.
  3. Which of Porter's generic strategies would be most effective given the competitive landscape?
  4. How could Lin evaluate potential diversification or integration opportunities using the GE/McKinsey matrix?
  5. What retrenchment or growth strategies should be considered for different product lines?
  6. How can Lin balance stakeholder expectations (environmentally-conscious consumers, retail partners, employees) in strategy development?

Analysis Framework

  • Step 1: External Analysis
    Market structure (pure competition, monopolistic competition, oligopoly, monopoly), industry attractiveness, PESTLE factors, opportunities & threats
  • Step 2: Internal Analysis
    Resources & capabilities, value chain position, competitive advantages, strengths & weaknesses
  • Step 3: Strategy Formulation
    Generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus), growth options (integration, diversification), corporate-level decisions
    • Key insight: The most successful strategies aren't just theoretically sound but practically implementable given the organization's reality.

    Corporate Strategy Options

    Strategy Type Description When to Consider
    Horizontal Integration Acquiring or merging with competitors To increase market share, eliminate competition, achieve economies of scale
    Vertical Integration Expanding into upstream/downstream activities To secure supply chain, increase margins, control quality
    Diversification Expanding into new products/markets To reduce risk, leverage capabilities in new areas, find growth
    Retrenchment Reducing business scope/scale To eliminate underperforming areas, focus on core strengths
    Remember: The McKinsey/GE Matrix helps evaluate which business units to grow, hold, or harvest based on market attractiveness and competitive strength.

    Evaluating Strategic Options

    Suitability

    • Addresses key strategic issues
    • Aligns with organizational mission
    • Leverages competitive advantages
    • Responds to market opportunities/threats

    Feasibility

    • Financial resources available
    • Necessary skills and capabilities
    • Operational implementation possible
    • Technology requirements manageable

    Acceptability

    • Meets stakeholder expectations (employees, customers, suppliers, etc.)
    • Acceptable financial returns for level of risk
    • Compatible with organization's culture and values
